Annotated Bibliography

Brandstetter, L., &amp; Lehner, O. M. (2014, November 9). Impact investment portfolios: Including Social Risks and returns. SSRN. Retrieved October 13, 2022, from https://ssrn.com/abstract=2519671

Previous literature has presented the certain barriers for institutional investors from engaging in social investing. Standardized measurement metrics are needed so as to holistically evaluate the qualitative impact of investments. The question arises of whether to maximize the impact or minimize the risk through ESG funds. A new three dimensional efficient frontier has to be developed to take into account not only the risk v return but also the impact. Another decision making by the investor is whether to achieve these objectives through direct investment where portfolio decision is on the project level or indirect investment whether portfolio decision is on the asset class level.

Grim, D., Renzi-Ricci, G., &amp; Madamba, A. (2022). Asset allocation with ESG preferences: Efficiently blending value with values. SSRN Electronic Journal. https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4176634

When determining the optimal portfolio, the non-monetary desires of the investor along financial expectations/risk aversion are key points to consider. Intuitively, there exists a tradeoff that is supported by evidence between financial and non-financial preferences. An optimal asset allocation would incorporate the level of risk aversion as well as the quality and degree of ESG features. Being able to customize these inputs would create a transparent tradeoff between the two. The analysis goes on to illustrate the differences between different levels of taste for ESG funds, different levels of equity fund “ESG-ness” and different levels of active risk aversion.

Oikonomou, I., Platanakis, E., &amp; Sutcliffe, C. (2018). Socially responsible investment portfolios: Does the optimization process matter? The British Accounting Review, 50(4), 379–401. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bar.2017.10.003

The article examines Socially Responsible Investment portfolios through six different approaches. The results are compared across the following parameters: risk, risk-return tradeoff, diversification and stability. Contradictory to conventional portfolios, Black Litterman and Markowitz have lower risk and higher risk-return trade-offs yet at the same time more unstable asset allocations and lower diversification. The optimization methods are as follows: Markowitz portfolio optimization, robust estimation, Black-Litterman, Naive diversification, Risk-parity portfolio and reward-to-risk timing portfolios.

Rehman, R. ur, Abidin, M. Z., Ali, R., Nor, S. M., Naseem, M. A., Hasan, M., &amp; Ahmad, M. I. (2021). The integration of conventional equity indices with environmental, social, and governance indices: Evidence from emerging economies. Sustainability, 13(2), 676. https://doi.org/10.3390/su13020676

        A key insight from this article came from its application in emerging economies. ESG equity indices are compared with its conventional counterparts in Brazil, Russia, India, China and South Africa individually as well as together through their regional cooperation from BRICS. A Johansen Cointegration test is applied to test the integration of ESG. One byproduct of this analysis is the confirmation of financial cooperation between BRICS countries.

Zuber, P. (2017). Risk, Return, Responsibility–Inclusion of ESG Criteria in a Portfolio Optimization Framework. https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2009.35.2.061

        A comprehensive document over a hundred pages, it begins on the subject of quantitative integration of responsibility in portfolio construction following a short literature review on responsible investment. A multi-objective optimization is the strategy used to achieve both profit and ESG goals. Modern Portfolio Theory core idea outlined by Markowitz is diversification of multiple risk assets for interrelated returns. The Black Litterman allows the investor view, a conditional distribution to be integrated with the prior distribution of the implied equilibrium return vector for a posterior distribution. A barrier as observed from the empirical evidence is whether this paradigm is sufficient for ESG criteria. Two methods to consider ESG scores are either for each variance entry to be from the maximum ESG score or the mean/median of the set. By overcoming this barrier, it provides stability in the weights over time.

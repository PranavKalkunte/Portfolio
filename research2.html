Annotated Bibliography

Da Silva, A. S., Lee, W., &amp; Pornrojnangkool, B. (2009). The black–litterman model for active portfolio management. The Journal of Portfolio Management, 35(2), 61–70.

https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2009.35.2.061

Active management seeks out alpha - the return of the portfolio - while minimizing the risk - the standard deviation of said alpha. While the Black-Litterman model was derived from the mean-variance which does in fact seek to carry out the goal of active management, it may lead to unintentional trades. The cause of this is centered around the fact that the model does not account for cases where we do not provide investment views which may lead to a mismatch. The paper goes on to provide an alternative method of implementing the Black-Litterman framework to increase the information ratio.

He, G., &amp; Litterman, R. (2002). The intuition behind Black-Litterman model portfolios. SSRN Electronic Journal. https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.334304

Behind the numerous pages of matrix manipulation and Bayesian statistics, the Black-Litterman model is meant to be a rather intuitive concept asserts the author. There is a direct correlation between the weights of an investor in comparison to the one implied by the market and how it

affects the weighted sum of the portfolio itself. For example, if the investor is more positive than the equilibrium, so is the weight of the portfolio itself. How confident the investor is on said view also correlates with the magnitude of the weight. Nonetheless, the paper goes on to define multiple equations and its application in an unconstrained optimal portfolio. Numerous numerical examples are provided as thus.

Idzorek, T. (2007). A step-by-step guide to the black-litterman model. Forecasting Expected Returns in the Financial Markets, 17–38. https://doi.org/10.1016/b978-075068321-0.50003-0

3

Perhaps the most useful publication, this article provides a step-by-step guide to the Black-Litterman. By reviewing previous literature, it derives the model for the combined return distribution. Additionally, the paper provides a new method for incorporating user-specified confidence levels. A formerly complicated model is turned into an intuitive framework that realizes the returns of the Markowitz paradigm while overcoming the weakness of mean-variance optimization. Satchell, S., &amp; Scowcroft, A. (2000). A demystification of the black–litterman model: Managing quantitative and traditional portfolio construction. Journal of Asset Management, 1(2), 138–150. https://doi.org/10.1057/palgrave.jam.2240011

4

The premise of the paper presents the conflict between traditional portfolio managers and their quantitative counterparts. While managers may be successful, it creates a disharmony for risk management and portfolio analysis. On the other hand, simple procedures such as mean-variance analysis do not seem to cover the full scope of the value of traditional management. One middle ground between the two is the Black-Litterman model which can incorporate both approaches as it utilizes investor views into a quantitative model. The model itself rests upon the foundation of Bayesian analysis, a way to analyze how a degree of belief - a probability - can be used to make inferences.

Walters, J. (2011). The black-litterman model in detail. SSRN Electronic Journal.

https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.1314585

A key insight provided by this paper is the taxonomy of different Black-Litterman models. It chronologically does a survey of previous papers on the subject to show how different concepts have evolved. It classifies models into three categories, the Canonical Reference Model, the Alternative Reference Model and the Beyond Black-Litterman Reference Model. Furthermore are the different ways to specify the views and the estimation model used. For our purposes, Meucci (2006) and Meucci (2008) were extensions for non-normal views and parameters besides expected return. This provides an interesting avenue of application to alternative investments.

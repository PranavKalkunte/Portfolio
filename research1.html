Annotated Bibliography

Friede, Gunnar, et al. “ESG and Financial Performance: Aggregated Evidence from More than 2000 Empirical Studies.” Journal of Sustainable Finance & Investment, vol. 5, no. 4, 2015, pp. 210–233., https://doi.org/10.1080/20430795.2015.1118917.

        This second level meta-analysis searches for a correlation between environmental, social, and governance (ESG) criteria and corporate financial performance (CFP). Two thousand two hundred empirical studies, of which ninety percent report positive findings, make this study an exhaustive review. Outlined is the data methodology by vote-count and meta-analysis for best-evidence synthesis. ESG outperformance is found in North America, Emerging Markets and non equity asset classes as opposed to capital markets which show no ESG-CFP relation. While long term responsible investing should be a fiduciary duty, the integration process of value-enhancing ESG factors will affect the performance impacts for CFP. When separating regions, non portfolio studies and young asset classes,  conclusions presented more favorable data. The comprehensive study must be noted that it was published in 2015 yet the historical data traced from the 1970s can still hold value.

Giese, Guido, et al. “Performance and Risk Analysis of Index-Based ESG Portfolios.” The Journal of Index Investing, vol. 9, no. 4, 2019, pp. 46–57., https://doi.org/10.3905/jii.2019.9.4.046.

        While there is extensive research regarding the financial benefits of ESG investing, this paper analyzes the integration methodology of environmental, social and governance (ESG) factors for asset management. The results of the article come from passive investing ESG indexes to address the options to integrate an ESG score. There are two index methodologies components - an ESG signal and an index integration methodology such as best-in-class selection and weight-tilt. The following allocations are analyzed - global markets, emerging markets, world ex usa and us regional index-based. There is a reduced risk in key measures for the Morgan Stanley Capital International (MSCI) ESG Leaders Indexes over the seven-year period from August 31, 2010 to December 31, 2017.

Gillan, Stuart L., et al. “Firms and Social Responsibility: A Review of ESG and CSR Research in Corporate Finance.” Journal of Corporate Finance, vol. 66, 2021, p. 101889., https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcorpfin.2021.101889.

        From 2011 to 2018, the percentage of S&P 500 firms releasing sustainability reports increased from under 20% to 86% as well as a 4 times increase of net flows for mutual funds with ESG mandates from 2018 to 2019 alone. The study considers the risk profile to ESG/CSR characteristics in relation to a firm performance. The main characteristics reviewed include the market, leadership and owners of a firm. Note that corporate social responsibility (CSR) is used interchangeably with ESG in the article. Their focus is the key factors of governance’s relationship with environmental and social considerations. The study itself does not seem to provide any robust conclusions but rather simply poses questions for further research. The premise of the paper seems to limit the literature coverage to be evaluated leaving its review lacking.

Halbritter, Gerhard, and Gregor Dorfleitner. “The Wages of Social Responsibility — Where Are They? A Critical Review of ESG Investing.” Review of Financial Economics, vol. 26, no. 1, 2015, pp. 25–35., https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rfe.2015.03.004.

        As the title suggests, this study presents a critical view of ESG investing. It assesses the link between corporate financial performance (CFP) and corporate social performance (CSP). It utilizes three ratings provided by ASSET4, Bloomberg and KLD. ESG portfolio method and cross-sectional regression are two strategies in the spirit of previous literature. The conclusion determines that there is no evidence for a significant relationship in all three portfolios based on the three data sets. The conclusion compares the empirical results and methodology employed in relation to the previous studies. While it may not provide strong financial benefit, the final sentence of the study does agree that social responsibility is a worthwhile investment category.

Nagy, Zoltán, et al. “Can Esg Add Alpha? an Analysis of ESG Tilt and Momentum Strategies.” The Journal of Investing, vol. 25, no. 2, 2016, pp. 113–124., https://doi.org/10.3905/joi.2016.25.2.113.

        Study focuses on two strategies - ESG title and ESG momentum. ESG tilt overweight stocks with higher ratings while ESG momentum overweights ESG rating improvement. The result of the article is that they outperformed global indexes. Performance seems proportional to either ESG score or change in ESG score. The global model was compared to the Morgan Stanley Capital International World Index. The appendix outlines the data sources, rick exposure and risk management analysis, key issue scores and weights and ESG ratings. ESG tilt portfolio tended towards lower-volatility stocks and the momentum portfolio tended towards positive price momentum.
